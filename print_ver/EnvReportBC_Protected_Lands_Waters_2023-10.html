Conserved Lands & Waters in B.C.

  • Conserved lands and waters are legally established areas set aside to protect nature and cultural values. Conserved areas, particularly BC Parks, are well-used by British Columbians with 6 in 10 people visiting a provincial park each year1.
  • Conserved lands and water cover 19.6% of B.C.’s terrestrial areas and inland water and 24.5% of coastal and marine areas. B.C.’s network of conserved lands and waters contribute to the Convention on Biological Diversity Aichi Target 11 commitments to set aside 17% of terrestrial areas and 10% of marine areas by 2023 (was previously 2020).
  • B.C.’s conserved areas include national and provincial parks and protected areas (PPAs) and other effective area-based conservation measures (OECMs).
  • For terrestrial areas/inland water, the conserved areas are comprised of 15.5% PPAs and 4.1% OECMs.
  • Marine areas in B.C. are comprised of 6.2% PPAs and 18.2% OECMs.
  • The amount of conserved lands and water is not evenly distributed across B.C. The following provides a glance at the distribution of conserved areas across B.C. and the representation of different ecosystems.

Trends in the Conservation of British Columbia’s Terrestrial and Marine Ecoregions

  • B.C. is divided into ecoregions. Ecoregions are considered ‘big picture’ ecosystems and group nearby areas with similar climate and geologic features together.
  • There are 40 terrestrial, 4 marine, and 2 ecoregions with marine and terrestrial components across B.C.
  • The most conserved terrestrial ecoregions are located in the northwest corner of B.C. - St. Elias Mountains and Chugach Mountains and Icefields are both 99% protected as PPAs.
  • The least conserved terrestrial ecoregions are the Pelly Mountains (1.2%) and the Peace River Basin (2.6%).
  • The most conserved marine ecoregion is the Transitional Pacific (51.1%), which consists of 49% OECM. The least conserved is the marine component of the Georgia Puget Basin (4.6%).

Terrestrial Ecosystem Representation within Conserved Lands in B.C.

  • The distribution of conserved areas is also shown by biogeoclimatic zones (BEC).
  • BEC zones are defined by the terrestrial ecosystems that develop in an area due to the climate and geography. They provide insight into the soils and plant communities you would expect to see in an area.
  • Conserved lands are not evenly distributed among British Columbia’s ecosystems. High elevation and mountainous areas such as the Boreal Altai Fescue Alpine and the Interior Mountain-heather Alpine zones are over 30% conserved as PPA’s (4 and 6% OECM respectively) while representing less than 7% of the province’s ecosystems.
  • By contrast, low elevation, warm zones such as Coastal Western Hemlock are more common across B.C. and tend to be less conserved (< 15% of BEC zone). Coastal Douglas-fir is the least conserved at 2% PPA.

Details of Conserved Land & Water Designations in B.C.

  • BC Parks (14.49% terrestrial; 0.98% marine)

    • Most of the protected land and a third of the protected waters in B.C. are managed by BC Parks.
    • Protected areas managed by BC Parks are governed by several pieces of legislation and fall into one of the following designations: Ecological Reserve, Provincial Park, Protected Area, Conservancy, Recreation Area.

  • National Protected Areas (0.65% terrestrial; 5.21% marine)

    • The Government of Canada has established protected land and marine areas in British Columbia, for example National Parks, National Wildlife Areas, Marine Protected Areas and Migratory Bird Sanctuaries.
    • These designations are governed by several pieces of federal legislation including: Canada National Parks Act, Canada National Marine Conservation Areas Act, Canada Wildlife Act, Migratory Birds Convention Act, and the Oceans Act (1996).

  • B.C. Provincial Administered Conservation Lands (4.32% terrestrial; 18.28% marine)

    • Administered Conservation Lands are lands over which the Government of British Columbia has legal administrative and management authority.
    • Wildlife Management Areas are Administered Conservation Lands that are designated under the provincial Wildlife Act to protect habitat for regionally, nationally or internationally significant species.
    • Other types of Administered Conservation Lands are aquired by way of: Land Act section 106 Transfers of Administration, Fee Simple Acquisitions (through direct purchase, donation, or land exchange), and Lease Sites.

  • B.C. Non-Government Organization Conservation Areas (0.12% terrestrial)

    • B.C. Non-Government Organization Conservation Areas, also known as private conservation lands, include Fee Simple areas, defined as ownership of the title to a legal parcel, and Registerable Interests — other conservation securements registered on title, such as conservation covenants and leases.

Methods

  • This indicator uses data from the Canadian Protected and Conserved Areas Database. The Old Growth Management Areas - Legal - Current and the Wildlife Habitat Areas datasets from DataBC were also used to fill in missing dates.
  • Municipal and regional parks are not included in the analyses. Overlaps between protected areas have been accounted for in the analyses.
  • The R code for repeating the analyses presented on this page is available on GitHub.
